How can we critically investigate the impact of the Internet, web interfaces, and software on culture, society, and politics? How can we redefine and take back our digital spaces to encode our own social and intimate spheres to create social change?

  • / 3 October - 29 October 2016

  • / four weeks, full-time in Barcelona, Spain

  • / 10-15 participants accepted

  • / course conducted in English

  • / Based in HANGAR


APPLY


This course will focus on critically exploring the internet and software for its social and political impact, while learning the technical tools to intervene in purposeful ways. Instead of simply accepting and consuming the technologies presented to us, we'll critically investigate the intentions and effects of these technologies, and ways they can be harnessed or exploited for meaningful social change.

Experiments with re-articulating interfaces at Poetic Deconstruction workshop, 2016. (Joana Moll)

Who is this program for?

This program is for artists, designers, writers, makers, coders, journalists and researchers interested in understanding, exploring and disrupting what's behind web interfaces. We will also investigate the underlying intentions of user interfaces, and how the UI designs demonstrate the latent political, socioeconomic, emotional, and cultural [opinions / tendencies / desires] of their designers.

Course Description

Facebook Demetricator is a web browser addon that hides notification numbers. No longer is the focus on how many friends you have or on how much they like your status, but on who they are and what they said. (Ben Grosser)

During this four-week long program, we will creatively reveal, deconstruct and re-articulate the role and influence of the internet and software on society by looking at examples of subversive/disruptive web practices and net hacktivism.

We will consider ways of using the web as a medium for activism, social change, political commentary while learning the technical tools required to creatively intervene - an urgent exercise to build technological paradigms which can more meaningfully respond to our current environmental and human conditions.

"DEFOOOOOOOOOOOOOOREST" shows the trees needed to counteract Google CO2 emissions x second, 2016. (Joana Moll)

We will look also into the potential impacts on security and privacy, as well as consider the role and impact of artificial intelligence and machine-learning.

We're excited to collaborate with our friends, the Influencers, whose yearly conference in Barcelona brings together unconventional art, guerrilla communication, and radical entertainment. As part of the program, we'll be attending their conference (Oct. 20-22) together.

Tracing You presents a website’s best attempt to see the world from its visitors’ viewpoints. (Ben Grosser)

What you will walk away with

  • A deeper understanding and awareness of the subliminal effects of the internet, software, interfaces and interaction choices of the tools we interact with everyday

  • A technical understanding of how websites and webpages are rendered, and how they can be manipulated through easily accessible browser-based tools

  • Hands-on experience with programming languages and tools for building your own web-based work - HTML, CSS, Javascript and JQuery

  • Practical knowledge for how to organize data, scrape websites, and classify and retrieve documents from large corporations.

  • Parsing, organizing, extracting, visualizing, and retrieving text from internet discussion forums such as newsgroups/blogs using python-based machine learning techniques.

  • Final exhibition of your work in Barcelona.


CO2GLE is a real-time, net-based installation that displays the amount of CO2 emitted on each second thanks to the global visits to Google.com. (Joana Moll)

Instructors

  • Joana Moll
    / janavirgin.com

    Joana Moll is a Barcelona based artist and a researcher. Her work critically explores the way post-capitalist narratives affect the alphabetization of machines, humans and ecosystems. Her main research topics include communication technologies and CO2 emissions, virtual civil surveillance on the Internet and language.

    She is a member of the scientific and artistic committee of the transdisciplinary research project Antiatlas des Frontières and co-founder of The Institute for the Advancement of Popular Automatisms. She is a visiting lecturer at College of Art and Design of Vic and a lecturer at VIT Lab in Vic (Barcelona).

  • Ben grosser / bengrosser.com/

    Artist Ben Grosser focuses on the cultural, social, and political implications of software. Recent exhibition venues include Eyebeam in New York, The White Building in London, Galerie Charlot in Paris, and Museum Ludwig in Cologne. His works have been featured in Wired, The Atlantic, The Guardian, Al Jazeera, and Der Spiegel. The Chicago Tribune called him the “unrivaled king of ominous gibberish.” Slate referred to his work as “creative civil disobedience in the digital age.”

    Grosser’s recognitions include First Prize in VIDA 16, the Expanded Media Award for Network Culture from Stuttgarter Filmwinter, and a Rhizome commission. He is an assistant professor at the University of Illinois and a faculty affiliate at the National Center for Supercomputing Applications. Prior to his current appointments, he earned an MFA in new media and an MM in music composition (both from Illinois), and was director of the Imaging Technology Group at the Beckman Institute.

Bani also teaches about digital cultures and social change at the University of Barcelona, where he is also a PhD candidate in Advanced Studies in Art Production.
